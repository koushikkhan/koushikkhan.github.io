Introduction

In our high school mathematics, we have learnt Differential Calculus, which has introduced the term ‘differentiation’ or ‘derivative’ of a function with respect to a variable. This derivative has played a significant role in today’s deep learning algorithms.

We all are familiar with the concept of derivative and hopefully, have solved a lot of exercises as well.

I this post, I will revisit the core idea of computing derivative and also try to develop the idea that is used behind the efficient learning process of a neural network, which is also known as back propagation.

Derivative of a function

Let us take a simple function \(f(.)\) that depends on the input \(x\). The derivative of \(f(x)\) w.r.t \(x\) is defined as follows:

\[ \frac{df}{dx} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{(x+h) - x} \]

The animation below, made with geogebra, will make the definition easier to understand.

Once you open this, you will see there are two sliders controlling:

  • the coordinate of the point \(A\) and
  • the amount of change \(h\) (only positive now) to put on point \(A\).

If you make \(h\) very close to zero, you will understand how the line segment \(\overline{PQ}\) gradually becomes the tangent of the curve at point \(A\).

As you understand, the ratio \(\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{(x+h) - x}\) is nothing but the tangent or \(tan()\) of the angle \(\angle{QPP'}\) from the triangle \(\triangle{QPP'}\).

Theoratically, when \(h\) is very close to zero, the ratio gives you the value of the tangent of the curve at the point \((x,0)\).

Therefore, the overall definition of derivative of a function, geometrically refers to the tangent value of the curve \(y = f(x)\) at the point \(x\).

The tangent of a curve at some point \(x\) is important as it gives us an idea about the nature of the curve locally. Geometrically, if the tangent line makes an acute angle with the x-axis (which is the case here), then it denotes a positive change on the output and when it makes an obtuse angle, it denotes a negative change on the output. If the tangent line horizontal to the x-axis, then there is no change on the output.

Similar information can also be obtained by evaluating the expression of derivative at some point \(x\). If the limiting value of the ratio is positive, then obviously, there is a positive change on the output otherwise the change is negative and having a value as zero indicates there is no change on the output.

Note that, till now, we have discussed only on a scalar valued function with a scalar valued input. We will now try to get some ideas to calculate derivative when either of the function and input or both of them are vector valued.

Derivative of vector valued functions

Functions of type \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}\)

Such functions take vector (s) as input (s) and return a scalar. Loss functions like MSE or Cross Entropy can be couple of examples.

In this case, derivative of \(f(\vec{x})\) will also be a vector of the same dimension, as given below:

\[ \frac{df}{d\vec{x}} = \left(\frac{df}{dx_1}, \ldots, \frac{df}{dx_n}\right) \]

This vector is often referred to as gradient vector.

Functions of type \(f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m\)

Such functions take vector (s) as input (s) and return another vector with different dimension as output.

A simple example can be a function \(f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3\):

  • input: \(\vec{x} = \left(x_1, x_2\right)\) and a matrix of parameters \(\mathbf{W}_{3 \times 2}\)
  • output: \(\vec{y} = \mathbf{f(\vec{x})} = \left(w_{11}x_1+w_{12}x_2, w_{21}x_1+w_{22}x_2, w_{31}x_1+w_{32}x_2)\right)\)

In this case, derivative of \(\mathbf{f(\vec{x})}\) will be a matrix of dimension \(m \times n\), as given below:

\[\begin{align} \frac{d\vec{y}}{d\vec{x}} &= \left(\frac{d\vec{y}}{dx_1}, \ldots, \frac{d\vec{y}}{dx_n}\right) \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \frac{dy_1}{dx_1} & \frac{dy_1}{dx_2} & \ldots & \frac{dy_1}{dx_n} \\ \frac{dy_2}{dx_1} & \frac{dy_2}{dx_2} & \ldots & \frac{dy_2}{dx_n} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{dy_m}{dx_1} & \frac{dy_m}{dx_2} & \ldots & \frac{dy_m}{dx_n} \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{align}\]

This matrix is often referred to as Jacobian of \(\vec{y}\) with respect to \(\vec{x}\).

The chain rule of differentiation

Consider the situation, where you have two different functions \(f()\) and \(g()\) defined as follows \(y = f(x)\) and \(z = g(y) = g\left(f(x)\right)\).

While forming \(z\), \(f()\) is the inner function and \(g()\) is the outer function. This is known as function composition as often denoted as \(g \circ f(x)\).

We want to measure the rate of change in \(z\) w.r.t \(x\) i.e. \(\frac{dz}{dx}\).

This derivative is computed as follws:

\[ \frac{dz}{dx} = \frac{dz}{dy} \times \frac{dy}{dx} \]

and it is known as the chain rule of differentiation.

Wikipedia has an intutive explanation as given below:

Understanding chain rule

Intuitively, the chain rule states that knowing the instantaneous rate of change of \(z\) relative to \(y\) and that of \(y\) relative to \(x\) allows one to calculate the instantaneous rate of change of \(z\) relative to \(x\) as the product of the two rates of change.

As put by George F. Simmons: “if a car travels twice as fast as a bicycle and the bicycle is four times as fast as a walking man, then the car travels 2 × 4 = 8 times as fast as the man.”

This chain rule is very important as it powers the back propagation heavily which will be discussed in the next section.

The computational graph

A computational graph is a directed acyclic graph that keeps track of the sequence of operations performed during the run time of an algorithm.

Let us take an example to understand it.

graph LR
  a(a) --> c(c = a+1)
  a(a) --> d(d = a+b)
  b(b) --> d(d = a+b)
  c(c = a+1) --> e(e = c * d)
  d(d = a+b) --> e(e = c * d)
  e(e = c * d) --> l(l = e+f)
  f(f) --> l(l = e+f)

The above diagram is typically referred to as a computational graph, where both a and b are inputs to the graph and e is the output of the graph.

Each of the circles in the graph is called a node where a mathematical operation is performed. So, if you imagine, deep neural network is actually such a massive computational graph where, depending on the depth of the network, millions (or may be billions) of mathematical operations are performed.

Derivative through a graph

Neural networks learn (i.e. update the parameters) using an iterative process where at the core it uses back propagation algorithm to compute derivative of a loss function.

Let us revisit the previous computational graph:

graph LR
  a(a) --> c(c = a+1)
  a(a) --> d(d = a+b)
  b(b) --> d(d = a+b)
  c(c = a+1) --> e(e = c * d)
  d(d = a+b) --> e(e = c * d)
  e(e = c * d) --> l(l = e+f)
  f(f) --> l(l = e+f)

Here, we can assume \(l\) as the loss and we want to measure the rate of change w.r.t each of the other nodes in the backward direction (why backward? … I’ll explain later).

This whole computation is performed in multiple steps as follows:

  • Step 1: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(l\) itself i.e. \(\frac{dl}{dl} = 1\)
  • Step 2: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(e\) i.e. \(\frac{dl}{de} = \frac{dl}{de} \times \frac{dl}{dl} = \frac{d(e+f)}{de} \times \frac{dl}{dl} = 1\)
  • Step 3: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(f\) i.e. \(\frac{dl}{df} = \frac{dl}{df} \times \frac{dl}{dl} = \frac{d(e+f)}{df} \times \frac{dl}{dl} = 1\)
  • Step 4: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(c\) i.e. \(\frac{dl}{dc} = \frac{de}{dc} \times \frac{dl}{de} = \frac{d(c*d)}{dc} \times \frac{dl}{de} = d\)
  • Step 5: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(d\) i.e. \(\frac{dl}{dd} = \frac{de}{dd} \times \frac{dl}{de} = \frac{d(c*d)}{dd} \times \frac{dl}{de} = c\)
  • Step 6: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(a\) i.e. \(\frac{dl}{da}\), this step can be broken into two parts as changing \(a\), can change \(l\) either through \(c\) or through \(d\), therefore,
    • \(\frac{dl}{da} = \underbrace{\left( \frac{dc}{da} \times \frac{dl}{dc} \right)}_{\text{changes through c}} + \underbrace{\left( \frac{dd}{da} \times \frac{dl}{dd} \right)}_{\text{changes through d}} = d + c\)
  • Step 7: rate of change of \(l\) w.r.t \(b\) i.e. \(\frac{dl}{db} = \frac{dd}{db} \times \frac{dl}{dd} = 1 \times c = c\)

The real benefit of computing derivatives (a.k.a gradients) in the backward direction is that it allows the learning algorithm to compute gradients for all the nodes with a single attempt. If we would have started in the forward direction, then the learning algorithm would have travelled the entire graph for each of the input nodes at the very beginning and this is the computational victory.

Pytorch, being most popular deep learning toolkit, has implemented a .backward() method to compute the derivatives for all the nodes (technically not all, only for them with requires_grad=True) in a computational graph with a single attempt.

Important takeways

When we have a \('+'\) operation in the graph, like the one below,

graph LR
  x(x) --> c(z = x+y) -.-> x(x)
  y(y) --> c(z = x+y) -.-> y(y)

computing gradients with respect to the input nodes is fairly simple as the backward gradient computation just copies the gradient of the output node to all the input nodes.

However, when we have a \('\times'\) operation in the graph, like the next one,

graph LR
  x(x) --> c(z = x*y) -.-> x(x)
  y(y) --> c(z = x*y) -.-> y(y)

gradient of one input node is just the product of the gradient of output node and value of other input node.

Note

\(+\) and \(\times\) are the two building blocks powering up any mathematical computation.

Sorting nodes before performing back propagation

graph LR
  a(a) --> c(c = a+b)
  b(b) --> c(c = a+b)

If we observe the above graph, it clear that while performing back propagation, computing gradient of any of the leaf nodes i.e. \(\left\{a, b\right\}\) is possible only when gradient value for the node \(c\) is already available. This is just because of the chain rule we use.

So the fact is that we cannot choose a node randomly and try computing gradient for it. We must compute the gradient of node \(c\), which is essentially 1, then we can take either of node \(a\) or node \(b\).

Nodes must be sorted in order to perform back propagation. Topological Sort is an way to achieve this ordering before performing the back propagation.

The efficient vector-jacobian product

Consider our tiny network:

  graph LR
  x1(x1) --> y1(y1)
  x2(x2) --> y1(y1)
  x1(x1) --> y2(y2)
  x2(x2) --> y2(y2)
  x1(x1) --> y3(y3)
  x2(x2) --> y3(y3)
  y1(y1) --> o(l)
  y2(y2) --> o(l)
  y3(y3) --> o(l)

It has only two input nodes, a single output node and in between them three additional nodes forming the hidden layer.

The mapping from input layer to hidden layer is supported by a matrix of parameters \(\mathbf{W}_{3 \times 2}\) and ofcourse by a vector valued linear function, say, \(\mathbf{\vec{f}}\).

Hidden layer values are formed using the equation below:

\[ \begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} w_{11} & w_{12} \\ w_{21} & w_{22} \\ w_{31} & w_{32} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{pmatrix} \]

and consider some differentiable scalar valued function \(g()\) that combines hidden layer values to form the output \(l\).

Computing \(l\) from the given fixed set of input values and with the help of some values of the parameters, is called the forward pass.

At this stage, we can definitely compute the jacobian matrix of \(\mathbf{\vec{f}}\) w.r.t \(\vec{x}\), which is given below:

\[ \mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{dy_1}{dx_1} & \frac{dy_1}{dx_2} \\ \frac{dy_2}{dx_1} & \frac{dy_2}{dx_2} \\ \frac{dy_3}{dx_1} & \frac{dy_3}{dx_2} \end{pmatrix} \]

Now, suppose \(\vec{v}\) is the gradient vector of \(l\) i.e. \(\vec{v} = \left(\frac{dl}{dy_1}, \frac{dl}{dy_2}, \frac{dl}{dy_3}\right)\)
